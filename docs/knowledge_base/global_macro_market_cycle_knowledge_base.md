## 1. Executive summary

Financial markets are not governed by a single cycle. They are shaped by several overlapping cycles:

1. **The business cycle**: real economic activity, production, employment, income, consumption, investment, and profits.
2. **The monetary / liquidity cycle**: central-bank policy, real rates, liquidity, quantitative tightening/easing, funding costs, and the availability of capital.
3. **The credit / financial cycle**: leverage, bank lending, credit spreads, collateral values, property prices, refinancing conditions, and default risk.
4. **The earnings / margin cycle**: revenues, pricing power, costs, operating leverage, margins, inventories, and analyst revisions.
5. **The valuation / risk-premium cycle**: the price investors are willing to pay for uncertain future cash flows.
6. **The commodity / inventory cycle**: supply-demand imbalances, inventories, capacity, capex discipline, geopolitics, and physical bottlenecks.
7. **The policy / geopolitical / structural cycle**: fiscal policy, regulation, industrial policy, trade policy, wars, technology transitions, demographics, and energy transitions.

The key investor principle is that markets often move before the visible economy turns. They discount expected changes in future cash flows, interest rates, inflation, policy response, and risk premia. This means that markets can rally while economic data still looks weak, and sell off while current earnings still look strong.

A robust macro radar should therefore not ask only: “Is the economy strong or weak?”  
It should ask:

- Is growth accelerating or decelerating?
- Is inflation rising, falling, sticky, or volatile?
- Are central banks easing, pausing, tightening, or constrained?
- Are financial conditions loosening or tightening?
- Is credit expanding or contracting?
- Are earnings expectations rising or falling?
- Are valuations cheap, fair, expensive, or extreme relative to rates and history?
- Are markets pricing a benign scenario while vulnerabilities are rising?
- Are sector/subsector fundamentals aligned with the macro phase, or distorted by structural factors?

The most important dashboard design principle is to separate:

- **Nowcasting**: What is happening now?
- **Leading indicators**: What may happen next?
- **Market pricing**: What is already discounted?
- **Vulnerability indicators**: What could amplify a shock?
- **Regime indicators**: What kind of environment are we in?
- **Confidence indicators**: How reliable is the signal right now?

No single indicator is sufficient. Reliable cycle analysis requires triangulation across growth, inflation, policy, liquidity, credit, earnings, valuation, market breadth, and sector behavior.

## 2. What is a market cycle?

A market cycle is a recurring, but not perfectly regular, sequence in which economic fundamentals, financial conditions, investor psychology, valuations, earnings, liquidity, and risk appetite move through recognizable phases.

Cycles are not clocks. They vary in duration, amplitude, and order. They can be interrupted by shocks. They can be extended by policy. They can become distorted by valuation extremes, leverage, geopolitics, or structural change.

A useful distinction:

| Term | Meaning | Investor relevance |
|---|---|---|
| **Cycle** | Recurrent pattern in economic or financial conditions | Helps frame probability and risk |
| **Regime** | More persistent background environment | Determines which signals work or fail |
| **Shock** | Discrete unexpected event | Can reset or accelerate cycles |
| **Trend** | Long-term structural force | Can dominate or mask cycles |
| **Noise** | Short-term volatility without durable signal | Must be filtered out |

### 2.1 Business cycle definition

The National Bureau of Economic Research defines US recessions and expansions by identifying peaks and troughs in broad economic activity. Expansions run from trough to peak; recessions run from peak to trough. The NBER emphasizes a broad assessment of depth, diffusion, and duration, not simply two negative quarters of GDP.

**Source:** NBER Business Cycle Dating  
https://www.nber.org/research/business-cycle-dating

### 2.2 Leading-indicator definition

The OECD Composite Leading Indicators are designed to provide early signals of turning points in business cycles, specifically fluctuations of activity around its long-term potential. The OECD stresses that CLIs provide qualitative information about turning points rather than precise quantitative forecasts.

**Source:** OECD Composite Leading Indicators  
https://www.oecd.org/en/data/datasets/oecd-composite-leading-indicators-clis.html

### 2.3 Financial-cycle definition

The BIS financial-cycle literature typically focuses on the joint behavior of credit and property prices. Financial cycles tend to be longer and larger in amplitude than business cycles, often extending over 8–30 years, compared with shorter business-cycle fluctuations.

**Sources:**  
BIS, Borio, *The financial cycle and macroeconomics: What have we learnt?*  
https://www.bis.org/publ/work395.pdf  

BIS, *The financial cycle and recession risk*  
https://www.bis.org/publ/qtrpdf/r_qt1812g.htm

## 3. Why cycles exist

Cycles exist because economies and markets are adaptive systems with delayed feedback loops. Households, companies, banks, governments, central banks, and investors all react to changing conditions, but not instantly and not perfectly.

### 3.1 Human behavior and expectations

Investors and businesses extrapolate recent conditions. Optimism increases after good outcomes; pessimism increases after bad outcomes. This creates procyclicality:

- Good growth improves confidence.
- Confidence increases spending, hiring, borrowing, capex, and risk-taking.
- Increased activity supports profits and asset prices.
- Higher asset prices improve collateral and perceived wealth.
- Easier financing enables more activity.
- Eventually, excesses build.

The reverse happens in downturns.

### 3.2 Credit and leverage

Credit turns ordinary business fluctuations into financial cycles. When lenders are optimistic, credit standards ease and leverage rises. When losses appear, standards tighten and refinancing becomes harder. This amplifies booms and busts.

This is why credit and property-price cycles matter. They influence both the real economy and financial stability.

### 3.3 Inventories and capacity

Companies respond to demand with a lag. They may overproduce when demand slows or underproduce when demand accelerates. Inventory cycles are especially important in manufacturing, semiconductors, autos, shipping, chemicals, metals, retail, and energy.

Typical pattern:

1. Demand rises.
2. Companies raise orders and production.
3. Inventories are rebuilt.
4. Demand slows.
5. Inventories become excessive.
6. Orders are cut.
7. Production falls more than final demand.
8. Inventories normalize.
9. Orders recover.

### 3.4 Policy reaction functions

Central banks and governments respond to inflation, unemployment, financial stress, and political constraints. Policy actions affect future growth, inflation, liquidity, and asset valuations, often with long and variable lags.

The key question is not only “what is policy?” but “what can policymakers do?”  
For example:

- In a disinflationary slowdown, central banks can usually ease.
- In a stagflationary slowdown, central banks may be constrained.
- In a debt-heavy economy, fiscal policy may be constrained.
- In a banking crisis, liquidity provision may dominate normal rate policy.

### 3.5 Valuation and risk premia

Asset prices are discounted expectations of future cash flows. They change because expected cash flows change, but also because discount rates and risk premia change.

John Cochrane’s asset-pricing work emphasizes that discount-rate variation is central to modern asset-pricing questions. This is highly relevant to cycle analysis because market prices can move sharply even when current cash flows change little.

**Source:** Cochrane, *Discount Rates*  
https://www.nber.org/system/files/working_papers/w16972/w16972.pdf

## 4. The main cycle framework

## 4.1 Early-cycle / recovery phase

### Macro characteristics

- Growth is weak but improving.
- Leading indicators bottom.
- PMIs recover from contraction.
- Financial conditions loosen.
- Credit spreads narrow.
- Central banks are easing or expected to ease.
- Inventories are low or being rebuilt.
- Earnings expectations are depressed but stabilize.
- Unemployment may still be rising because labor is lagging.

### Market characteristics

- Equities often rally before GDP and employment clearly recover.
- High beta, cyclicals, small/mid caps, credit, and distressed assets may outperform.
- Yield curves may steepen.
- Bond yields may rise if growth expectations improve.
- Risk appetite improves.

### Why it happens

Markets respond to the change in direction. If growth is still poor but no longer deteriorating, forward earnings expectations and risk appetite can improve rapidly.

### Useful indicators

- PMI new orders rising from low levels.
- OECD CLI turning upward.
- Credit spreads narrowing.
- Yield curve steepening after inversion.
- Bank lending standards stabilizing.
- Equity breadth improving.
- Earnings revisions turning less negative.
- Inventory-to-sales ratios peaking.
- Initial jobless claims stabilizing.

### Common false signals

- Bear-market rallies during a continuing recession.
- Policy easing that fails to restart credit creation.
- Temporary inventory rebound without final-demand recovery.
- Markets rallying on rate cuts while earnings continue to collapse.

---

## 4.2 Mid-cycle / expansion phase

### Macro characteristics

- Growth is positive and broadening.
- Employment improves.
- Consumption and investment are healthy.
- Credit is available.
- Inflation is usually contained or moderate.
- Central banks move toward neutral policy.
- Earnings growth is positive but less explosive than in early recovery.

### Market characteristics

- Equity returns can remain positive.
- Leadership broadens, then becomes more selective.
- Quality and earnings delivery matter more.
- Credit returns normalize.
- Volatility tends to be lower.

### Why it happens

The economy has recovered enough to become self-sustaining. Policy is no longer doing all the work. Profits grow through real activity rather than only multiple expansion.

### Useful indicators

- PMI around or above 50 with stable new orders.
- Industrial production and capacity utilization improving.
- Earnings revisions positive.
- Credit spreads stable.
- Yield curve normal or moderately flat.
- Inflation expectations anchored.
- Consumer confidence and labor income improving.
- Capex orders rising.

### Common false signals

- Strong coincident data late in the cycle can look mid-cycle.
- Equity markets can appear stable while leadership narrows.
- Low volatility can create complacency.

---

## 4.3 Late-cycle / overheating phase

### Macro characteristics

- Growth is still positive but decelerating.
- Labor markets are tight.
- Wage pressure rises.
- Inflation becomes sticky or broad.
- Central banks tighten or stay restrictive.
- Yield curves flatten or invert.
- Credit conditions tighten.
- Margins face pressure from wages, interest costs, and input costs.
- Capex and inventories may overshoot.

### Market characteristics

- Leadership narrows.
- Defensives and quality often outperform.
- Valuations become more vulnerable.
- Credit spreads may begin widening.
- Long-duration assets can struggle if real rates rise.
- Commodity sectors may still perform if inflation is supply-driven.

### Why it happens

The expansion creates constraints: tight labor, high capacity utilization, rising costs, excess leverage, or inflation. Policy shifts from supporting growth to restraining inflation or financial excess.

### Useful indicators

- Unemployment below estimated neutral levels.
- Wage growth high relative to productivity.
- Core inflation sticky.
- Central banks restrictive.
- Yield curve inverted.
- Credit spreads no longer tightening.
- Bank lending standards tightening.
- Earnings revisions rolling over.
- Profit margins peaking.
- Inventory accumulation above demand.
- Market breadth deteriorating.

### Common false signals

- Late-cycle can last longer than expected.
- Strong nominal growth can support earnings even as real growth slows.
- Commodity/inflation winners may mask weakness elsewhere.
- An inverted yield curve can lead recession by many months or longer.

---

## 4.4 Recession / contraction phase

### Macro characteristics

- Output contracts or stagnates.
- Employment weakens.
- Profits decline.
- Capex is cut.
- Inventories are liquidated.
- Credit losses rise.
- Defaults increase.
- Inflation may fall, unless supply shocks persist.
- Central banks may ease if inflation allows.

### Market characteristics

- Equities are vulnerable, especially cyclical and leveraged companies.
- Credit spreads widen.
- Government bonds may rally if inflation falls.
- Defensive sectors outperform.
- Cash flow, balance-sheet quality, and liquidity matter most.
- Market bottoms often occur before the recession officially ends.

### Why it happens

Demand falls, profits contract, and financing becomes harder. If leverage is high, forced deleveraging amplifies the downturn.

### Useful indicators

- PMIs below 50.
- Initial claims rising.
- Payroll growth slowing or negative.
- Industrial production falling.
- Credit spreads widening.
- Lending standards tightening.
- Earnings revisions negative.
- Defaults rising.
- Consumer confidence weak.
- Housing and durable-goods demand falling.

### Common false signals

- Official recession dating is backward-looking.
- Markets may bottom while macro data still deteriorates.
- Defensive performance can persist even after the economic trough if credit remains impaired.
- Rate cuts are not always bullish if they occur because earnings and credit are collapsing.

## 5. The “sequence” of a typical cycle

A simplified sequence often seen in markets:

1. Liquidity and policy expectations turn.
2. Financial conditions change.
3. Market prices and credit spreads move.
4. Leading indicators turn.
5. Earnings revisions turn.
6. Real activity turns.
7. Employment turns.
8. Official recession/expansion dating confirms the move much later.

This sequence is not fixed. In inflation shocks, policy may lag or fight the cycle. In financial crises, credit stress can dominate. In geopolitical shocks, commodity prices and supply chains can lead the cycle.

## 6. Major cycle drivers

## 6.1 Growth

Growth drives revenues, utilization, labor demand, investment, and operating leverage. Investors should separate:

- Level of growth: strong or weak.
- Rate of change: accelerating or decelerating.
- Composition: consumption, investment, exports, government, inventories.
- Quality: real growth versus inflation-driven nominal growth.
- Breadth: broad-based or concentrated.

### Key indicators

| Indicator | What it shows | Use |
|---|---|---|
| Real GDP | Broad activity | Lagging/confirming |
| PMIs | Timely business momentum | Leading/coincident |
| Industrial production | Real output | Cyclical sectors |
| Retail sales | Consumer demand | Consumption cycle |
| Housing starts/permits | Rate-sensitive demand | Early-cycle signal |
| Capex orders | Investment cycle | Industrial cycle |
| Freight/shipping | Goods activity | Global trade cycle |
| OECD CLI | Turning points | Leading, qualitative |

---

## 6.2 Inflation

Inflation determines whether weak growth is good news or bad news for markets.

- If inflation falls with slowing growth, central banks can ease.
- If inflation stays high while growth slows, policy support is constrained.
- If inflation rises because demand is strong, cyclicals may initially benefit.
- If inflation rises because supply is impaired, margins and real incomes may suffer.

### Types of inflation

| Inflation type | Typical source | Market implication |
|---|---|---|
| Demand-pull | Excess demand | Supports nominal revenues but invites tightening |
| Cost-push | Energy, wages, supply chain | Margin pressure |
| Wage-price | Labor scarcity | Sticky and policy-sensitive |
| Asset inflation | Liquidity and credit | Valuation and financial stability risk |
| Imported inflation | FX, commodities, tariffs | Country/sector specific |

### Key indicators

- Headline CPI / PCE
- Core CPI / PCE
- Trimmed mean and median inflation
- Wage growth
- Unit labor costs
- Inflation expectations
- Commodity prices
- Freight rates
- Supplier delivery times
- Rent and shelter inflation

---

## 6.3 Interest rates and real yields

Interest rates affect discount rates, mortgage rates, corporate financing costs, bank margins, FX, duration assets, and equity valuations.

Real yields are especially important because equities are claims on real future cash flows. Higher real yields increase the opportunity cost of owning risky assets and can pressure long-duration growth equities.

### Key indicators

- Central-bank policy rate
- 2-year yield
- 10-year yield
- Yield-curve slope
- Real yields / TIPS yields
- Term premium estimates
- Mortgage rates
- Corporate borrowing costs

---

## 6.4 Liquidity and financial conditions

Liquidity affects the availability and price of capital. It is broader than central-bank policy rates.

The Chicago Fed’s National Financial Conditions Index provides a weekly update on US financial conditions across money markets, debt and equity markets, and traditional and shadow banking systems. Positive NFCI values indicate tighter-than-average conditions; negative values indicate looser-than-average conditions.

**Source:** Chicago Fed NFCI / FRED  
https://fred.stlouisfed.org/series/NFCI

### Key indicators

- Financial conditions indexes
- Credit spreads
- Equity volatility
- Dollar liquidity
- Repo and funding stress
- Bank reserves
- Central-bank balance sheets
- Money-market stress
- Cross-currency basis swaps
- Market depth / liquidity indicators

## 6.7 Valuation and expected returns

Valuation is generally more useful for long-horizon expected returns than for short-term timing.

Campbell and Shiller found that valuation ratios such as dividend-price and smoothed earnings-price ratios have historically contained information about long-run returns, while being much less reliable for near-term forecasts.

**Source:** Campbell & Shiller, *Valuation Ratios and the Long-Run Stock Market Outlook*  
https://www.nber.org/system/files/working_papers/w8221/w8221.pdf

### Common valuation metrics

| Metric | Use | Limitations |
|---|---|---|
| P/E | Simple equity valuation | Distorted by cyclically low/high earnings |
| Forward P/E | Market expectations | Analyst estimates can be wrong |
| CAPE | Cycle-adjusted valuation | Accounting changes, sector mix, rates matter |
| EV/EBITDA | Capital-structure neutral | Ignores capex intensity |
| P/B | Banks, insurers, asset-heavy firms | Less useful for intangible-heavy firms |
| FCF yield | Cash-generation focus | Cyclical capex can distort |
| Equity risk premium | Relative valuation vs bonds | Model-sensitive |
| Dividend yield | Income and valuation | Buybacks reduce comparability |
| Credit spread | Risk compensation in credit | Liquidity and technicals matter |

## 7. Sector and subsector cycle behavior

The sector cycle should be treated as probabilistic. Sector performance depends not only on the macro phase but also on starting valuation, earnings revisions, sector composition, balance sheets, regulation, geopolitics, and structural trends.

The Global Industry Classification Standard (GICS) is a widely used global classification system with four levels: sectors, industry groups, industries, and sub-industries. It is useful for dashboard taxonomy and sector/subsector mapping.

## 7.1 Energy

### Key subsectors

- Integrated oil & gas
- Exploration & production
- Oilfield services
- Refining & marketing
- Midstream / pipelines
- LNG
- Renewables / clean energy equipment

### Main drivers

- Oil and gas prices

- Global demand growth
- Inventories
- Spare capacity
- Rig count and capex
- Refining margins
- Geopolitical supply risk
- USD
- Energy transition policy

### Cycle behavior

Energy often performs well in late-cycle inflationary environments when demand is strong and supply is constrained. It may underperform in recessions if demand collapses. Oilfield services are more capex-cycle sensitive than integrated majors. Refiners depend heavily on crack spreads, not simply crude prices. Midstream assets may be more defensive if contracts are fee-based.

### Dashboard indicators

- Natural gas prices
- Global oil inventories
- OPEC spare capacity
- IEA/EIA demand forecasts
- Rig count
- Crack spreads
- LNG spot prices
- Energy sector earnings revisions
- Capex guidance
- Free cash flow yield

### Reliability notes

Energy is highly exposed to geopolitical shocks and policy decisions, making purely statistical cycle models fragile.

## 11. Reliability and validation rules

## 11.1 Never rely on one indicator

A robust radar should require evidence across multiple categories.

Example: a recession warning is more reliable if the following align:

- Leading indicators deteriorate.
- Yield curve is inverted or has re-steepened after inversion.
- Credit spreads widen.
- Lending standards tighten.
- Earnings revisions fall.
- Labor data weakens.
- Market breadth deteriorates.
- Financial conditions tighten.

## 11.2 Separate signal from noise

Monthly indicators are volatile. Use:

- 3-month change
- 6-month change
- Year-over-year change
- Z-scores
- Percentile ranks
- Diffusion across indicators
- Confirmation across regions/sectors

## 11.3 Beware revised data

Some macro indicators are revised materially. Real-time data can tell a different story than final data. This is especially important for GDP, payrolls, industrial production, and leading indexes.

## 11.4 Distinguish hard data and surveys

Surveys are timely but subjective. Hard data is more objective but lagged. The best framework uses both.

## 11.5 Watch for regime changes

Historical relationships can fail when:

- Inflation regime changes.
- Central banks change reaction function.
- Fiscal dominance becomes relevant.
- Globalization reverses.
- War or sanctions reshape supply chains.
- Technology changes sector economics.
- Index composition changes.
- Accounting standards change.

## 11.6 Use market prices carefully

Markets are forward-looking but not always correct. A market rally can signal recovery, but it can also reflect positioning, liquidity, short covering, or excessive optimism.

## 11.7 Valuation is not timing

High valuation increases vulnerability and lowers long-run expected returns, but it does not reliably predict the next 3–12 months. Cheap assets can get cheaper if earnings collapse or credit tightens.

## 12. Conflicting views and important debates

## 12.1 Are cycles predictable?

**View 1: Partly predictable.**  
Leading indicators, yield curves, credit conditions, and earnings revisions contain useful information about cycle turning points.

**View 2: Not reliably predictable in real time.**  
Data revisions, shocks, false signals, policy changes, and market reflexivity make precise timing unreliable.

**Practical conclusion:**  
Use cycles for probability, risk management, scenario analysis, and sector emphasis — not mechanical forecasting.

---

## 12.2 Do yield curves still work?

**Pro-yield-curve view:**  
Yield-curve inversions have historically preceded many US recessions and summarize monetary tightness and future easing expectations.

**Skeptical view:**  
Quantitative easing, term-premium distortions, global savings, regulation, and safe-haven demand can distort the curve.

**Practical conclusion:**  
Use yield curves as one important signal, but require confirmation from credit, labor, PMIs, earnings, and financial conditions.

---

## 12.3 Is inflation good or bad for equities?

**Positive nominal-growth view:**  
Moderate inflation can support nominal revenues and earnings.

**Discount-rate/margin-pressure view:**  
High or volatile inflation raises discount rates, compresses margins, reduces real income, and constrains central banks.

**Practical conclusion:**  
Inflation is most dangerous when it is high, volatile, supply-driven, or forces restrictive policy during slowing growth.

---

## 12.4 Are sector rotations reliable?

**Pro-sector-rotation view:**  
Sectors have different sensitivities to growth, rates, inflation, credit, and commodity prices.

**Skeptical view:**  
Sector composition changes, valuations differ, and structural trends can overwhelm historical patterns.

**Practical conclusion:**  
Sector-cycle analysis is useful, but subsector and company-level drivers must be checked.

---

## 12.5 Are financial cycles more important than business cycles?

**Financial-cycle view:**  
Credit, leverage, and property prices drive major booms and busts; financial downturns can create severe recessions.

**Traditional business-cycle view:**  
Real activity, production, income, and employment remain the core cycle.

**Practical conclusion:**  
For investors, both are necessary. The business cycle drives earnings; the financial cycle determines amplification and crisis risk.

## 13. Practical cycle-state scoring concept

A knowledge-base dashboard can classify each major dimension:

| Dimension | State examples |
|---|---|
| Growth | Accelerating / stable / decelerating / contraction |
| Inflation | Falling / stable / rising / sticky |
| Policy | Easing / neutral / tightening / constrained |
| Liquidity | Loose / neutral / tight / stressed |
| Credit | Expanding / stable / tightening / impaired |
| Earnings | Up revisions / stable / down revisions / recessionary |
| Valuation | Cheap / fair / expensive / extreme |
| Risk appetite | Fear / neutral / optimism / euphoria |
| Market breadth | Broad / mixed / narrow / deteriorating |
| Vulnerability | Low / moderate / high / systemic |

A cycle radar should avoid producing a single overconfident answer. A better output is:

- Current state
- Direction of change
- Confidence level
- Supporting indicators
- Contradicting indicators
- Data freshness
- Main risks to interpretation

## 14. Example interpretation logic

### Example A: Soft landing / mid-cycle extension

- Growth slowing but positive.
- Inflation falling.
- Central bank can ease modestly.
- Credit spreads stable.
- Earnings revisions stable.
- Market breadth broad.
- Valuations not extreme.

Likely implications: balanced risk environment; quality cyclicals and selective growth may perform.

### Example B: Late-cycle inflation risk

- Growth positive but decelerating.
- Core inflation sticky.
- Wage pressure high.
- Policy restrictive.
- Yield curve inverted.
- Credit standards tightening.
- Margins peaking.

Likely implications: quality, defensives, cash-flow resilience, and lower leverage become more important.

### Example C: Recessionary deleveraging

- PMIs below 50.
- Claims rising.
- Credit spreads widening.
- Lending standards tight.
- Earnings revisions falling.
- Defaults rising.
- Policy easing begins, but credit transmission weak.

Likely implications: preserve capital; prefer strong balance sheets, defensives, duration if inflation permits.

### Example D: Early-cycle recovery

- Leading indicators bottom.
- Credit spreads narrow.
- Financial conditions loosen.
- Earnings revisions stabilize.
- Market breadth improves.
- Policy becomes supportive.

Likely implications: cyclicals, credit, small/mid caps, and operating leverage may recover.

## 15. What a good macro/market-cycle knowledge base should prevent

It should prevent:

- Treating one indicator as a full cycle signal.
- Confusing current strength with future strength.
- Confusing falling inflation with good news when caused by demand collapse.
- Assuming rate cuts are always bullish.
- Ignoring credit conditions.
- Ignoring valuation.
- Overgeneralizing sector behavior.
- Treating structural growth as immunity from cycles.
- Using revised historical data as if it were available in real time.
- Ignoring data lags and publication delays.
- Mixing facts, estimates, forecasts, and opinions without labels.

## 18. Final practical takeaway

A good macro-cycle radar is not a crystal ball. It is a disciplined way to organize uncertainty.

The most useful radar does four things:

1. Identifies the current macro and market regime.
2. Tracks the direction of change across independent indicators.
3. Separates facts, estimates, forecasts, and market pricing.
4. Highlights where the evidence conflicts.

The strongest cycle signal is not one indicator flashing red or green. It is the convergence of several independent indicators — growth, inflation, liquidity, credit, earnings, valuation, market internals, and sector behavior — all pointing in the same direction.

**Knowledge-base confidence assessment:** High confidence for the conceptual framework, source hierarchy, cycle definitions, and indicator categories. Moderate confidence for sector/subsector timing rules because these are historically useful but regime-dependent and vulnerable to valuation, policy, geopolitical, and structural distortions.
